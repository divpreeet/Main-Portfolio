been thinking about this a lot lately. game piracy — is it good or bad? short answer: yes.

## the bad

piracy is theft. plain and simple. developers spend years of their lives building something, and pirating it means they don't get paid. if you're a solo dev or a small studio, every sale matters. one pirated copy could be the difference between making rent and not.

there's also the entitlement problem. people act like they deserve games for free. you don't. if you can't afford it, that sucks, but that doesn't mean you're owed someone else's work.

and yeah, piracy can hurt discoverability. if everyone pirates your game, no one talks about it, no one reviews it, it just sinks.

## the good

but here's the thing — piracy isn't always black and white.

**preservation.** so many games are literally unplayable today because they're locked on dead platforms, delisted from stores, or require servers that don't exist anymore. piracy is sometimes the only way to experience gaming history.

**try before you buy.** i've pirated games, loved them, and then bought them. i never would have spent $60 on something i'd never heard of. in that sense, piracy is a demo that the industry stopped making.

**accessibility.** not everyone lives in a country where game prices make sense. $70 is a lot different in the US vs. brazil or india. regional pricing helps, but it doesn't fix everything.

**DRM is anti-consumer.** if i buy a game and it phones home to a server that's going to shut down in 5 years, did i really buy it? piracy gives you a version that actually works forever.

## where i land

i don't pirate games from small indie devs. simple as. if a team of 3 people made something i want to play, i pay for it.

big corpo games with anti-consumer practices? i have less sympathy. nintendo charging $60 for a decade-old port? square enix delisting games to remake them? denuvo tanking performance? yeah, i get why people pirate.

the real solution is better accessibility, better preservation, and fair pricing. until then, piracy's not going anywhere.
